• MOA: May modulate dopamine and serotonin; some MAO inhibitory action suspected.
• Pharmacokinetics: Onset 30–60 min, oral or alcoholic extract. Mild duration (~2–3 hours).
• Therapeutic Uses: Relaxation, aphrodisiac, mild euphoria, sleep aid.
• Side Effects: Drowsiness, dizziness, dry mouth.
• Contraindications: Avoid with MAOIs, sedatives, or alcohol.

Kava (Piper methysticum)
• MOA: Kavalactones act on GABA-A receptors; anxiolytic without impairing cognition.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; onset in 20–40 min, peak ~1–2 hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Anxiety, muscle relaxation, sleep aid.
• Side Effects: Liver strain, drowsiness, GI upset.
• Contraindications: Liver disease, alcohol use, barbiturates.
• Drug Interactions: Additive with other depressants.
• Toxicity: Hepatotoxicity possible with chronic high use.
Wild Dagga (Leonotis leonurus)
• MOA: Thought to bind weakly to cannabinoid receptors.
• Pharmacokinetics: Fast onset when smoked. Tea: 30–60 min.
• Therapeutic Uses: Mild euphoria, calming effects, visual enhancement.
• Side Effects: Dry mouth, mild sedation.
• Contraindications: Avoid mixing with strong sedatives.
• Drug Interactions: Synergistic with other CNS relaxants.
• Toxicity: Low toxicity profile.
Damiana (Turnera diffusa)
• MOA: Unknown; theorized to affect serotonin and dopamine.
• Pharmacokinetics: Tea or smoked; onset in 30–45 minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Aphrodisiac, mood enhancer, stress relief.
• Side Effects: Mild headaches or insomnia in high doses.
• Contraindications: Avoid during pregnancy or with hypoglycemic meds.
• Drug Interactions: May amplify stimulant or sedative effects.
• Toxicity: Safe in normal doses; excessive use may cause convulsions.
Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)
• MOA: Inhibits GABA breakdown; increases GABA availability.
• Pharmacokinetics: Active as tea or extract; onset 30–60 min.
• Therapeutic Uses: Anxiety, insomnia, nervous tension.
• Side Effects: Drowsiness, dizziness, confusion.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy, use with sedatives or MAOIs.
• Drug Interactions: Potentiates CNS depressants.
• Toxicity: Low, but high doses may depress the CNS.
Akuamma (Picralima nitida)
• MOA: Primarily acts on opioid receptors; akuammine is a weak mu-opioid agonist.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; effects begin in 30–60 minutes and last 3–5 hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Analgesic, antidiarrheal, mild sedative.
• Side Effects: Nausea, dizziness, dry mouth.
• Contraindications: Avoid in combination with other opioids.
• Drug Interactions: May potentiate CNS depressants.
• Toxicity: Low in moderate doses; high doses can lead to respiratory depression.
Bay Bean (Canavalia maritima)
• MOA: May contain weak serotonin-like compounds; unclear primary mechanism.
• Pharmacokinetics: Traditionally smoked; onset within minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Mild psychoactive effects, often used in ceremonial blends.
• Side Effects: Rare; possible mild GI upset.
• Contraindications: Avoid if allergic to legumes.
• Drug Interactions: May interact with serotonergic substances.
• Toxicity: Very low toxicity profile.
Mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris)
• MOA: Contains thujone; GABA-A receptor antagonist and mild hallucinogen.
• Pharmacokinetics: Smoked or taken as tea; onset in 20–40 minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Lucid dreaming, menstrual aid, digestive tonic.
• Side Effects: Headaches, nausea, dizziness in high doses.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy, epilepsy, or seizure disorders.
• Drug Interactions: May reduce effectiveness of anticonvulsants.
• Toxicity: Thujone is neurotoxic in high doses; avoid long-term overuse.
Skullcap (Scutellaria lateriflora)
• MOA: Enhances GABA binding; anxiolytic and sedative properties.
• Pharmacokinetics: Active as tea or extract; onset 20–30 min.
• Therapeutic Uses: Anxiety, insomnia, nervous tension.
• Side Effects: Drowsiness, confusion, potential liver enzyme elevation.
• Contraindications: Use caution with liver disease or sedatives.
• Drug Interactions: Additive with CNS depressants.
• Toxicity: Safe in moderate doses; hepatotoxicity possible with impure extracts.
Bala (Sida cordifolia)
• MOA: Contains ephedrine alkaloids; sympathomimetic effects (stimulant).
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; fast-acting stimulant, peak ~1–2 hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Energy boost, thermogenic, bronchodilator.
• Side Effects: Increased heart rate, anxiety, insomnia.
• Contraindications: Heart conditions, hypertension, anxiety disorders.
• Drug Interactions: Potentiates stimulants and MAOIs.
• Toxicity: High doses may lead to cardiovascular stress.
Moringa (Moringa oleifera)
• MOA: Contains flavonoids, isothiocyanates; antioxidant and neuroprotective effects.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally consumed; onset within 30–90 minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Cognitive support, anti-inflammatory, nutritional supplement.
• Side Effects: Rare; possible GI discomfort in large amounts.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy (high doses); consult physician if on medication.
• Drug Interactions: May lower blood sugar and blood pressure; interacts with diabetes meds.
• Toxicity: Low toxicity; excessive use may cause liver enzyme elevation.
Wild Lettuce (Lactuca virosa)
• MOA: Contains lactucin and lactucopicrin; thought to act on opioid receptors.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active as tea, tincture, or smoked; effects in 30–45 min.
• Therapeutic Uses: Mild analgesic, sedative, sleep aid.
• Side Effects: Drowsiness, dizziness, dry mouth.
• Contraindications: Avoid in pregnancy or if allergic to lettuce family.
• Drug Interactions: May potentiate CNS depressants and pain medications.
• Toxicity: Low to moderate toxicity in large doses; nausea and confusion possible.
Yohimbe (Pausinystalia johimbe)
• MOA: Alpha-2 adrenergic receptor antagonist; increases norepinephrine release.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; onset in 30–60 minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Aphrodisiac, stimulant, erectile dysfunction treatment.
• Side Effects: Increased heart rate, anxiety, hypertension, insomnia.
• Contraindications: Heart disease, hypertension, psychiatric disorders.
• Drug Interactions: Potentiates stimulants and MAOIs.
• Toxicity: High doses may cause seizures or cardiac issues.
Betel Nut (Areca catechu)
• MOA: Arecoline acts on muscarinic receptors; stimulant and parasympathomimetic.
• Pharmacokinetics: Chewed; rapid onset of stimulation.
• Therapeutic Uses: Mild stimulant, social use in Southeast Asia.
• Side Effects: Staining of teeth, dependence, mouth irritation.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy, heart conditions, psychiatric disorders.
• Drug Interactions: May interact with cholinergic and cardiovascular meds.
• Toxicity: Carcinogenic with long-term use; associated with oral cancer.
Salvia divinorum
• MOA: Potent kappa-opioid receptor agonist; hallucinogenic.
• Pharmacokinetics: Smoked or sublingual; onset in seconds to minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Visionary experiences, spiritual exploration.
• Side Effects: Disorientation, anxiety, dysphoria, altered time perception.
• Contraindications: Psychiatric disorders, operating machinery.
• Drug Interactions: May intensify with other dissociatives or CNS agents.
• Toxicity: Very low toxicity; intense effects can be psychologically overwhelming.
Kratom (Mitragyna speciosa)
• MOA: Acts on mu-opioid receptors; mitragynine is primary active alkaloid.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; onset in 15–45 min, lasts 3–6 hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Pain relief, mood boost, energy or sedation (dose-dependent).
• Side Effects: Constipation, nausea, dependency potential.
• Contraindications: Avoid mixing with other depressants or MAOIs.
• Drug Interactions: Potentiates opioids and sedatives.
• Toxicity: Low-to-moderate; long-term use can lead to dependence and withdrawal.
Heimia (Heimia salicifolia)
• MOA: Unclear; may affect acetylcholine and serotonin systems.
• Pharmacokinetics: Traditionally prepared as tea; onset in 1–2 hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Mild auditory alterations, tranquil state.
• Side Effects: Dry mouth, slowed cognition, dizziness.
• Contraindications: Avoid when driving or operating machinery.
• Drug Interactions: May interact with anticholinergic drugs.
• Toxicity: Low toxicity in ceremonial doses.
Celastrus paniculatus (Intellect Tree)
• MOA: Unknown; believed to modulate acetylcholine and monoamine neurotransmitters.
• Pharmacokinetics: Seeds consumed orally or as oil; onset ~1 hour.
• Therapeutic Uses: Cognitive enhancement, memory aid, antidepressant effects.
• Side Effects: Mild headache, GI upset, stimulation at high doses.
• Contraindications: None well-documented.
• Drug Interactions: May interact with MAOIs or nootropics.
• Toxicity: Generally safe in moderate doses.
Syrian Rue (Peganum harmala)
• MOA: Potent reversible MAOI (harmaline, harmine).
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; onset in 30–90 min, long duration (4–8 hrs).
• Therapeutic Uses: MAOI for ayahuasca analogues, lucid dreaming, meditation.
• Side Effects: Nausea, vomiting, tremors, serotonin syndrome (if misused).
• Contraindications: SSRI/SNRI/MAOI use, pregnancy, liver disease.
• Drug Interactions: Strongly potentiates serotonergic and psychedelic compounds.
• Toxicity: Moderate; can cause serotonin syndrome if mixed incorrectly.
Calea zacatechichi (Dream Herb)
• MOA: Unknown; may act on serotonin and cholinergic systems.
• Pharmacokinetics: Smoked or brewed; onset in 20–45 min.
• Therapeutic Uses: Dream enhancement, lucid dreaming.
• Side Effects: Bitter taste, nausea, vivid dreams, drowsiness.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy, avoid driving afterward.
• Drug Interactions: May potentiate sedatives.
• Toxicity: Low toxicity.
Guarana (Paullinia cupana)
• MOA: Contains caffeine and other xanthines; stimulates CNS via adenosine receptor antagonism.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; onset within 30–60 minutes, effects can last 4–6 hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Energy boost, cognitive enhancement, appetite suppression.
• Side Effects: Insomnia, jitteriness, elevated heart rate.
• Contraindications: Heart issues, anxiety, insomnia disorders.
• Drug Interactions: Stimulants, MAOIs, blood pressure meds.
• Toxicity: Low in moderation; high doses risk cardiac strain or anxiety.
Nutmeg (Myristica fragrans)
• MOA: Contains myristicin and elemicin; mild hallucinogenic effects via anticholinergic action.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active; onset delayed (3–6 hours), long duration (~12 hours).
• Therapeutic Uses: Deliriant, dream enhancement, traditional aphrodisiac.
• Side Effects: Nausea, dry mouth, anxiety, delirium, hallucinations.
• Contraindications: Psychiatric history, liver issues.
• Drug Interactions: CNS depressants, MAOIs.
• Toxicity: Moderate to high at psychoactive doses; avoid overdose due to potential toxicity.
African Dream Root (Silene capensis)
• MOA: Unknown; thought to enhance REM sleep and dream vividness.
• Pharmacokinetics: Traditionally consumed as cold water infusion; onset over hours.
• Therapeutic Uses: Lucid dreaming, spiritual rituals.
• Side Effects: Bloating, nausea if taken in large amounts.
• Contraindications: None documented.
• Drug Interactions: Unknown.
• Toxicity: Considered safe in traditional use.
White Sage (Salvia apiana)
• MOA: Contains eucalyptol, camphor; antimicrobial, mildly psychoactive when burned.
• Pharmacokinetics: Inhaled via smoke or used topically.
• Therapeutic Uses: Cleansing rituals, calming effect, antimicrobial.
• Side Effects: Irritation from smoke inhalation.
• Contraindications: Asthma, respiratory conditions.
• Drug Interactions: None significant.
• Toxicity: Very low.
Lion’s Mane (Hericium erinaceus)
• MOA: Stimulates NGF (nerve growth factor) via hericenones and erinacines.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally consumed; slow-building cognitive effects over weeks.
• Therapeutic Uses: Nootropic, neuroprotective, mood support.
• Side Effects: Mild GI upset in some individuals.
• Contraindications: Allergies to mushrooms.
• Drug Interactions: None well-known.
• Toxicity: Extremely low.
Reishi Mushroom (Ganoderma lucidum)
• MOA: Immunomodulatory, anti-inflammatory; triterpenoids may affect histamine release.
• Pharmacokinetics: Best as extract or decoction; cumulative effect.
• Therapeutic Uses: Stress reduction, immune support, adaptogen.
• Side Effects: Dry mouth, dizziness, GI upset.
• Contraindications: Bleeding disorders, pre-surgery.
• Drug Interactions: Anticoagulants, immunosuppressants.
• Toxicity: Very low; long-term use is generally safe.
Chaga (Inonotus obliquus)
• MOA: High in antioxidants; may modulate cytokines and reduce oxidative stress.
• Pharmacokinetics: Typically consumed as tea or extract.
• Therapeutic Uses: Immune booster, antioxidant, anti-inflammatory.
• Side Effects: Rare; may lower blood sugar.
• Contraindications: Diabetes medications, surgery.
• Drug Interactions: Immunosuppressants, anticoagulants.
• Toxicity: Very low.
Cordyceps (Cordyceps militaris/sinensis)
• MOA: May increase ATP production; acts on adrenal and immune systems.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally consumed; effects build with regular use.
• Therapeutic Uses: Energy enhancement, stamina, libido.
• Side Effects: Mild GI discomfort possible.
• Contraindications: Autoimmune conditions (theoretical).
• Drug Interactions: Immunosuppressive drugs.
• Toxicity: Very low toxicity.
Ma Huang (Ephedra sinica)
• MOA: Ephedrine is a potent sympathomimetic; increases norepinephrine release.
• Pharmacokinetics: Fast-acting stimulant; onset 20–40 min orally.
• Therapeutic Uses: Energy, thermogenesis, bronchodilation.
• Side Effects: Tachycardia, hypertension, anxiety.
• Contraindications: Heart disease, hypertension, anxiety disorders.
• Drug Interactions: Stimulants, MAOIs, beta blockers.
• Toxicity: Moderate to high; banned in some countries due to cardiac events.
Calamus (Acorus calamus)
• MOA: Contains beta-asarone; potential GABA modulating and anticholinergic effects.
• Pharmacokinetics: Tea or chewed root; onset in 30–90 min.
• Therapeutic Uses: Lucid dreaming, stomach tonic, mild hallucinogen.
• Side Effects: Nausea, sedation, toxicity with high doses of beta-asarone.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy, liver issues.
• Drug Interactions: Sedatives, alcohol.
• Toxicity: Moderate; beta-asarone is carcinogenic in high doses (found in some strains).
Gotu Kola (Centella asiatica)
• MOA: Modulates collagen synthesis, improves circulation, may influence GABA.
• Pharmacokinetics: Orally active as tea or capsule; takes days to weeks for cognitive benefits.
• Therapeutic Uses: Cognitive enhancer, wound healing, anxiety relief.
• Side Effects: Headache, nausea, skin irritation.
• Contraindications: Liver disease, pregnancy in high doses.
• Drug Interactions: Sedatives, diuretics, anticonvulsants.
• Toxicity: Low; liver strain with prolonged high-dose use.
Hops (Humulus lupulus)
• MOA: Contains xanthohumol and humulene; GABA-A modulator with sedative properties.
• Pharmacokinetics: Consumed in teas or extracts; onset 30–60 minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Insomnia, anxiety, restlessness.
• Side Effects: Drowsiness, hormonal effects, allergic reactions.
• Contraindications: Hormone-sensitive conditions, pregnancy.
• Drug Interactions: Sedatives, CNS depressants.
• Toxicity: Low.
Valerian Root (Valeriana officinalis)
• MOA: Enhances GABA transmission; sedative and anxiolytic.
• Pharmacokinetics: Onset 30–60 min; peak in 1–2 hrs.
• Therapeutic Uses: Insomnia, anxiety, nervous tension.
• Side Effects: Headache, dizziness, vivid dreams.
• Contraindications: Pregnancy, use with alcohol or sedatives.
• Drug Interactions: CNS depressants, barbiturates.
• Toxicity: Low; possible dependency with long-term use.
Indian Warrior (Pedicularis densiflora)
• MOA: Unknown; likely acts on musculoskeletal relaxation pathways.
• Pharmacokinetics: Smoked or tea; fast onset, short duration.
• Therapeutic Uses: Muscle relaxant, anxiety relief, dream enhancement.
• Side Effects: Drowsiness, mild dry mouth.
• Contraindications: None documented.
• Drug Interactions: Sedatives.
• Toxicity: Low.
Kanna (Sceletium tortuosum)
• MOA: Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI); may also affect PDE4.
• Pharmacokinetics: Sublingual, smoked, or oral; onset 15–60 minutes.
• Therapeutic Uses: Mood enhancement, social anxiety, focus.
• Side Effects: Headache, nausea, serotonin syndrome (if mixed).
• Contraindications: SSRIs, MAOIs, pregnancy.
• Drug Interactions: Antidepressants, stimulants.
• Toxicity: Low–moderate; safe alone, dangerous with SSRIs/MAOIs.
Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)

• Therapeutic Uses: Stress relief, anxiety, testosterone boost, sleep.
• Side Effects: GI upset, drowsiness, thyroid hormone changes.
• Contraindications: Thyroid disorders, pregnancy.
• Drug Interactions: Sedatives, thyroid meds.
• Toxicity: Low.
Tulsi (Ocimum sanctum)

• Therapeutic Uses: Stress reduction, cognitive clarity, respiratory support.
• Side Effects: None common; mild GI issues possible.
• Contraindications: Fertility concerns, blood sugar meds.

Damiana (Turnera diffusa) [Extended]

Rhodiola (Rhodiola rosea)

Maca (Lepidium meyenii)

Yerba Mate (Ilex paraguariensis)

Lobelia (Lobelia inflata)
